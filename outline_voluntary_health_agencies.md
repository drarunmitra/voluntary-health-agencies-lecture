**Slide 5 — Park's formal definition**
- *"An organisation administered by an autonomous board, which holds meetings, collects funds chiefly from private sources... in furthering public health, by providing health services or health education, or by advancing research or legislation for health, or by a combination of these activities."*
- *Note:* this is the exact examinable definition — have students copy it verbatim

**Slide 6 — Scale & the "motor truck" analogy**
- US had **>20,000 VHAs by 1945** — VHAs flourished most in the USA
- VHAs = **"motor trucks"** (can go by-ways, flexible) vs govt agencies = **"railway trunk lines"** (must run on tracks established by law)

**Slide 7 — Characteristics**
- Autonomous board; funded chiefly from private sources; may use paid or volunteer workers
- Registered (Societies Act/Trusts); FCRA governs foreign funding

**Slide 9 — SECTION: Park's Six Functions (a–f)**

**Slide 10 — (a) Supplementing the work of government agencies**
- Government cannot cope with the problem alone → VHAs strengthen it via personnel, funds, equipment, services

**Slide 11 — (b) Pioneering**
- Exploring new ways/means; when efforts succeed, government can adopt at scale
- **Example: family planning in India** — VHAs first spearheaded the movement despite opposition, before government adopted it as national policy

**Slide 12 — (c) Education**
- "Unlimited scope for health education" — government alone cannot cope without voluntary effort

**Slide 13 — (d) Demonstration**
- Putting up demonstrations & experimental projects
- **Classic example: Rockefeller Foundation's bore-hole latrine demonstration** to solve hookworm disease in India — its modifications later became a standard part of India's environmental sanitation programme

**Slide 14 — (e) Guarding the work of government agencies**
- By setting a good example, VHAs can guide *and* critique government work

**Slide 15 — (f) Advancing health legislation**
- VHAs mobilise public opinion and advance legislation on health matters for community benefit

**Slide 16 — SECTION: Indian VHAs (Park's roster — 12 listed)**

**Slide 17 — 1. Indian Red Cross Society (1920)**
- Established by an **Act of the Indian Legislature, 1920**; **400+ branches**
- Activities: (a) relief work — disasters/epidemics (b) milk & medical supplies to hospitals/MCH centres (c) armed forces welfare — "Red Cross Home," Bangalore (d) MCH welfare centres (e) family planning clinics (f) blood banks & first aid (St. John Ambulance)
- **Junior Red Cross** — active youth wing for village uplift, first aid, anti-epidemic work

**Slide 18 — International Red Cross Movement (context)**
- Founded by **Henry Dunant** after witnessing the Battle of **Solferino, 1859**
- **First Geneva Convention, 1864** → International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC)
- **League of Red Cross Societies, 1919** (Geneva) coordinates 90+ national societies

**Slide 19 — 2. Hind Kusht Nivaran Sangh (1950)**
- HQ New Delhi; precursor was the **Indian Council of the British Empire Leprosy Relief Association (BELRA)**, renamed LEPRA in 1950
- Financial assistance to leprosy homes/clinics, health education, training, research, All-India Leprosy Workers' Conferences, publishes *"Leprosy in India"*

**Slide 20 — 3. Indian Council for Child Welfare (1952)**
- Affiliated with the **International Union for Child Welfare**
- Network of state councils & district councils; secures children's rights to healthy physical, mental, moral, spiritual & social development

**Slide 21 — 4. Tuberculosis Association of India (1939)**
- Branches in all states; annual **TB Seal campaign** for funds
- Manages: New Delhi TB Centre, Lady Linlithgow Sanatorium (Kasauli), King Edward VII Sanatorium (Dharampur), TB Hospital (Mehrauli)

**Slide 22 — 5. Bharat Sevak Samaj (1952)**
- Non-political, non-official; branches in all states/most districts
- Focus: sanitation improvement in villages

**Slide 23 — 6. Central Social Welfare Board (1953)**
- Autonomous, under Ministry of Education
- Functions: (1) survey needs of voluntary welfare orgs (2) promote/set up welfare orgs (3) financial aid to deserving orgs
- Started "Family & Child Welfare Services" (1968) — craft teaching, literacy, maternity aid, balwadis

**Slide 24 — 7. Kasturba Memorial Fund (1944)**
- Created in commemoration of Kasturba Gandhi's death; corpus of nearly ₹1 crore (historic)
- Improves rural women's lives through gram-sevikas

**Slide 25 — 8. Family Planning Association of India (1949)**
- HQ Mumbai; **pioneered** family planning work in India (links to Function b)
- Runs FP clinics with government grants-in-aid; trains doctors/health visitors/social workers

**Slide 26 — 9. All India Women's Conference (1926)**
- Only nationwide women's voluntary welfare organisation at the time
- Runs MCH clinics, medical centres, adult education centres, milk centres, FP clinics

**Slide 27 — 10. All-India Blind Relief Society (1946)**
- Coordinates institutions working for the blind; organises eye relief camps

**Slide 28 — 11. Professional bodies**
- **Indian Medical Association, All India Licentiates Association, All India Dental Association, Trained Nurses Association of India**
- Conduct conferences, publish journals, set professional standards, organise relief camps in calamities

**Slide 30 — SECTION: International & bilateral agencies (Ch. 25)**

**Slide 31 — Rockefeller Foundation (1913)**
- Chartered by John D. Rockefeller; India work began **1920** — hookworm control, Madras Presidency
- Helped establish **All India Institute of Hygiene & Public Health, Kolkata**; trained teachers/researchers; supports AIIMS-linked rural training

**Slide 32 — Ford Foundation**
- Focus: rural health services & family planning (vs Rockefeller's university/postgrad focus)
- India projects: orientation training centres (Singur, Poonamalle, Najafgarh); NIHAE (Delhi); Gandhigram rural health pilot (Tamil Nadu); Calcutta water/drainage scheme

**Slide 33 — CARE (1945)**
- Cooperative for Assistance & Relief Everywhere; founded post-WWII, North America
- India operations from **1950**; initially child feeding (6–11 y); from mid-1980s — ICDS support, anaemia control, women's health & reproductive health projects
- Active across AP, Bihar, MP, Maharashtra, Odisha, Rajasthan, UP, West Bengal

**Slide 34 — Colombo Plan (1950)**
- Commonwealth Foreign Ministers' meeting, Jan 1950; cooperative economic development, South/SE Asia
- 20 developing + 6 non-regional members; AIIMS New Delhi received Colombo Plan support; fellowships & equipment (e.g., cobalt therapy units)

**Slide 35 — SIDA & DANIDA (bilateral)**
- **SIDA** (Sweden) — assisting National TB Control Programme since 1979 (X-ray units, microscopes, anti-TB drugs)
- **DANIDA** (Denmark) — supporting National Blindness Control Programme since 1978

**Slide 36 — Modern additions beyond Park's classic list**
- **Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation** — major contemporary philanthropic funder in global/Indian health (not in Park's original text, but essential current knowledge)
- **VHAI** (Voluntary Health Association of India) — Park's text lists it among NGOs; current sources describe it as the **apex coordinating body**, federation of 27 state VHAs
- *Note:* flagged separately from the textbook-examinable list above

**Slide 37 — Crucial distinction**
- **Voluntary/philanthropic/bilateral** (Rockefeller, Ford, CARE, SIDA, DANIDA) ≠ **inter-governmental** (WHO, UNICEF, UNFPA, World Bank — separate chapter in Park's)

**Slide 38 — Role in NHPs & coordination**
- Family planning, TB, leprosy, blindness control — direct historical VHA/bilateral contributions shown above
- Modern accountability note: FCRA compliance remains a live governance issue (e.g., 2024 licence cancellations) — present neutrally

**Slide 39 — Key takeaways**
- Park's definition + six functions (a–f) are core exam content
- Indian VHA roster has specific founding years — high-yield SAQ material
- Distinguish voluntary/bilateral from inter-governmental agencies
